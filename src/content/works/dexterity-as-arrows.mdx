## The Purity-Safety Connection

Here is a deceptively simple observation: if your dynamics simulator is a **pure function** — no hidden state, no side effects — then a safety shield that calls it as a one-step oracle gets a prediction *identical* to what actually happens. The model mismatch is zero. Not approximately zero. **Exactly zero.** By referential transparency.

This paper builds **FP-dex**, a system that turns this observation into a practical safety framework for stochastic manipulation policies.

## Arrowized Controller Composition

Drawing from the functional reactive programming (FRP) tradition, we structure the controller as a composition of **Arrow signal functions**:

```
controller = dup >>> first policy >>> swap >>> shield
```

Policy, shield, and plumbing are all pure stream transformers composed via `>>>`, `first`, `arr`. All state is frozen algebraic data; transitions are pure functions; stochasticity is confined to an explicitly threaded PRNG key.

## Three-Layer Predictive Shield

The safety system operates in three layers:

1. **Action magnitude cap** — hard-clips actions to a fraction of the physical limit
2. **Rate limiter** — bounds the maximum change between consecutive actions
3. **Predictive CBF layer** — runs the pure dynamics forward one step, checks force and velocity invariants, and uses binary search to find the largest safe action within the braking envelope

Because the prediction is *exact* (by purity), the inductive safety proof carries no accumulated error term.

## Formal Guarantees

We prove two main theorems:

- **Theorem 1 (Inductive Safety):** Force and velocity bounds hold for all time steps, with zero accumulated model error
- **Theorem 2 (Safety-Liveness Tradeoff):** We characterize the exact boundary where safety and progress become incompatible, parametrized by stiffness, mass, and force budget

## The Counterintuitive Result: Safety Improves Performance

Across 900 adversarial episodes:

| Metric | Baseline | Shielded | Shielded + Retuned |
|--------|----------|----------|-------------------|
| Success rate | 48.3% | 81.7% | **83.0%** |
| Violation rate | 100% | **0%** | 0.3% |
| Mean violations/episode | 182.4 | 0.0 | 0.01 |
| Mean completion steps | 247.7 | 148.5 | **151.2** |

The shield doesn't just prevent bad outcomes — it acts as an **implicit regularizer**, pruning high-variance trajectory tails and concentrating execution on paths with monotonic progress. Safety literally makes the policy faster.

## CEGAR-Style Policy Retuning

Property-based testing (in the QuickCheck tradition) generates adversarial counterexamples that the pure simulator can deterministically replay. A CEGAR-style loop identifies three archetype failure modes — "The Stiff Wall," "The Featherweight," and "The Tight Corridor" — and retunes the policy via grid search over gain and noise scales.

## Qualitative Validation

Beyond the 1D peg-in-hole benchmark, we demonstrate multi-object dexterous grasping in the **SAPIEN** simulator with a bimanual UR5e + XHand system across five object categories: bottles, bowls, cellphones, cameras, and headphones.
