## Overview

**SynPolDex** starts from a simple observation: dexterous manipulation is not just about moving joints well, but about assigning meaningful roles to different fingers. Some fingers lead; others stabilize, support, or stay clear. The project turns that intuition into a bi-level learning framework.

## Method

The method is split into two stages.

At the high level, a vision-language model proposes a finger-role plan for the current task and hand embodiment. It decides which fingers should dominate the manipulation and which should play supporting roles.

At the low level, reinforcement learning turns that plan into control. Two design choices matter here:

- reward shaping that encourages the right fingers to contact the right object parts
- advantage factorization that gives stronger learning signal to the joints that matter most for the chosen role assignment

## Generalization

We evaluate on three different robot hands: Allegro, Inspire, and Leap. This matters because finger coordination should not be tied to one embodiment. The same task can demand different role assignments when hand morphology changes.

## Results

Evaluated on the DexArt-m benchmark across four manipulation tasks:

| Task | Hand | Success Rate |
|------|------|-------------|
| Toilet lid | Allegro | **90%** |
| Faucet | Allegro | **82%** |
| Faucet | Leap | **76%** |
| Laptop lid | Allegro | **58%** |
| Bucket | Allegro | **56%** |
| Toilet lid | Inspire | **52%** |

In human-likeness evaluation, SynPolDex scores more than **2x higher** than the PPO baseline, showing that better role assignment also produces more interpretable behavior.
